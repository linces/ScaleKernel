# Scale Kernel

# 02 — Fundamental Axioms

**Version:** 0.1.0

---

# Purpose

This document defines the smallest known set of principles that currently constitute the conceptual kernel of Scale Kernel.

These axioms are not assumed to be final.

Their purpose is to provide the minimal conceptual structure from which the remainder of the framework can be derived.

Future research may reduce, replace, merge or eliminate any of these axioms.

The objective is always reduction rather than expansion.

---

# What is an Axiom?

Within Scale Kernel, an axiom is a principle that is temporarily accepted because every attempt to remove it causes explanatory loss.

An axiom is therefore not an unquestionable truth.

It is simply the smallest currently known building block.

Every axiom remains open to revision.

---

# Axiom 1 — Existence Through Interaction

A system only exists as a system because interactions occur between its components.

Without interaction, there is no organization.

Without organization, there is no system.

Interaction is therefore the minimum condition for systemic existence.

---

# Axiom 2 — Locality

Every interaction is local.

No individual agent possesses complete knowledge of the global state.

Global behavior emerges from the accumulation of local interactions.

---

# Axiom 3 — Distributed Causality

Every interaction produces consequences.

Those consequences propagate throughout the system, directly or indirectly.

Causality is therefore distributed rather than centralized.

No single agent governs the complete evolution of the system.

---

# Axiom 4 — Persistence of Information

Every interaction modifies the informational structure of the system.

These modifications become part of the system's history.

Information is therefore the persistent structural record of causal transformations.

---

# Axiom 5 — Recursive Feedback

The consequences generated by interactions influence future interactions.

The system continuously acts upon itself.

Feedback is therefore an intrinsic property of systemic evolution.

---

# Axiom 6 — Dynamic Role Alternation

Agents do not permanently occupy the roles of cause or consequence.

Roles continuously alternate as interactions propagate.

The distinction between actor and recipient is temporal rather than permanent.

---

# Axiom 7 — Emergent Self-Regulation

Global stability is not necessarily imposed.

It may emerge naturally from recursive causal interactions distributed across the system.

Order can therefore arise without centralized control.

---

# Axiom 8 — Continuous Evolution

A complex system never reaches complete informational rest while remaining active.

Its existence depends upon continuous structural transformation.

Static equilibrium represents the absence of further systemic evolution.

---

# Relationships Between the Axioms

The axioms form a dependency chain.

Interaction makes locality meaningful.

Locality enables distributed causality.

Distributed causality generates information.

Information enables feedback.

Feedback produces role alternation.

Role alternation enables self-regulation.

Self-regulation sustains continuous evolution.

Each principle reinforces the next.

Together they define the current Scale Kernel.

---

# Design Philosophy

The kernel deliberately contains as few assumptions as possible.

Every additional principle increases explanatory cost.

Whenever a new axiom becomes necessary, researchers should first attempt to derive it from existing ones.

Reduction always takes precedence over expansion.

---

# Criteria for Accepting New Axioms

A new axiom should only be introduced if all the following conditions are satisfied:

* It explains phenomena that cannot be derived from the current kernel.
* It does not contradict existing axioms.
* It increases explanatory power more than conceptual complexity.
* It cannot itself be derived from existing axioms.
* It remains applicable across multiple scales.

If any of these conditions fail, the proposed axiom should not be incorporated into the framework.

---

# Research Direction

One of the long-term goals of Scale Kernel is to determine whether this kernel can be compressed even further.

The ideal outcome is not to increase the number of axioms.

The ideal outcome is to discover that some of them are consequences of even deeper principles.

The strongest kernel is the smallest kernel capable of preserving explanatory power.

---

# Closing Statement

These axioms should be understood as the current foundation of the Scale Kernel Research Program.

They are intentionally provisional.

Their permanence depends entirely on their ability to survive criticism, empirical confrontation where applicable, and future conceptual refinement.

The ultimate objective of Scale Kernel is not to preserve its axioms, but to discover whether an even simpler and more elegant kernel exists.
